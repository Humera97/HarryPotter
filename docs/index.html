“Mr. and Mrs. Dursley, of number four Privet Drive, were proud to say that they were perfectly normal, thank you very much...”

...And so, the story of Harry Potter begins. Over 20 years ago, on a summer day, what was meant to be a simple fantasy series about an 11-year-old boy soon enraptured millions across the world, spawning parodies, musicals, plays, movies, and even a theme park. Within a few short years, a whole new universe was born.

From this Wizarding World one aspect, in particular, stood out. The movies quickly gained wide-spread success, reaching historic ticket sales and breaking box office records.

Weekly Box Office Sales

By the time the last movie was released, people were standing in line for hours, waiting for midnight premieres. Domestically, the franchise was selling hundreds of millions in tickets sales. Globally, the number touched a billion

One movie, however, stood out from the rest. Harry Potter and the Prisoner of Azkaban, the third franchise in the installment made the least amount of money.

The third movie marks the beginning of Harry's turn from his early years at Howgarts, to a darker, more serious journey ahead. From the fourth installment onwards, someone dies in everybook. The early installments, however, played a special role in this magical world: they introduced many of the people in Harry's close circle- his best friends, family, and mentors. In particular, the first few movies focused on the growing friendship between Harry and his two best friends, Ron and Hermionie. The two would later accompany him on almost every journey he undertook

Role of the "Golden Trio"

Harry, Ron, and Hermionie are the heart of the Harry Potter franchise. However, combined, they make up less than half of all the dialogue in the film.

Despite the public attention on certain characters, it is surprising to note the some of the people most associated with Harry Potter rarely appeared in the early stages. Voldemort said only a few lines in the Sorcerer's Stone, the first movie, and made no appearance in the Prisoner of Azkaban, the third movie. Similarly, despite Harry's fraught relationship with his cousin Dudley, he rarely said anything at all throughout the early movies.

It seems like the "supporting characters" may not have much of a role at all.

Looking at the main characters and the amount they spoke, I was struck by something: most of the people speaking seemed to be men. With the exception of Hermionie, most of the main female characters did not seem to have as many lines. In fact, there did not seem to be that many women in the series at all.

From villanous organizations to underground resistance networks, several groups makeup the Wizarding World of Harry Potter. But how inclusive are these groups to men and women-- or should I say, how inclusive are they to wizards and witches?
